MODULE 01

What is Scale?

Real objects are too large to draw at full size. Scale is the mathematical relationship between a drawing and the real thing.

🎯 The Big Idea
Scale is a ratio. It compares the size of your drawing to the actual size of the object. If a wall is 5 metres long in real life but only 5 centimetres on your drawing, the scale is 1:100 — the drawing is 100× smaller than reality.

Think of it like a map. A city map doesn't show every street at actual size — that would be as big as the city itself! Instead, every centimetre on the map might represent 1 kilometre of real ground. That's a scale of 1:100,000.

📖 How to Read a Scale Ratio
1 : 50 Drawing size : Real-world size

This means: 1 unit on paper = 50 units in real life.
The unit can be anything — mm, cm, inches — as long as both sides use the same unit.

🔽 Reduction Scale (most common)

The drawing is smaller than real life.
Example: 1:50, 1:100, ¼" = 1'
Used for buildings, site plans, maps.

🔼 Enlargement Scale

The drawing is bigger than real life.
Example: 5:1, 10:1
Used for tiny parts like circuit boards or mechanical components.

📐 The Scale Formula
Drawing Size = Real Size ÷ Scale Factor OR: Real Size = Drawing Size × Scale Factor

Example: A room is 6 metres wide. You're drawing it at 1:50.

  1. Convert to the same units: 6 m = 6000 mm
  2. Divide by scale factor: 6000 ÷ 50 = 120 mm
  3. So the room is drawn 120 mm wide on your paper

MODULE 02

Imperial vs Metric

Why does Canada use two measurement systems? And which one do you use when drafting? Let's find out.

🌍 A Quick History

Imperial System 🇺🇸🇬🇧

  • Developed in Britain, centuries old
  • Uses: inches, feet, yards, miles
  • Also: pounds, ounces, gallons
  • USA never switched — still dominant there
  • Canada used it exclusively until the 1970s

Metric System 🌍

  • France, 1790s — based on powers of 10
  • Uses: mm, cm, m, km
  • Also: grams, litres, Celsius
  • Canada officially switched in 1970
  • Used by 95% of the world's population
🇨🇦
Canada's situation: Canada officially uses metric, but construction and real estate still heavily use imperial (feet/inches). As a drafter in BC, you must be fluent in both.
📏 Unit Conversions You Must Know

⚠️
Key difference: Metric is base-10 (10 mm = 1 cm, 100 cm = 1 m). Imperial has no consistent base (12 inches = 1 foot, 3 feet = 1 yard, 1760 yards = 1 mile). This is why metric is easier to calculate with!

🔄 Imperial Scale Notation Explained

Imperial architectural scales look different from metric ratios. Here's how to decode them:

¼" = 1'-0" Means: ¼ inch on the drawing = 1 foot (1'-0") in real life

To convert this to a ratio: 1 foot = 12 inches, so 1'-0" = 12". Then ¼" to 12" is a ratio of 1:48 (since 12 ÷ 0.25 = 48). So ¼" = 1'-0" is almost exactly 1:50.

MODULE 03

Scale Tools

Physical scale rules look like triangular prisms with 6 flat faces — each face has a different scale printed on it. Here's how to read them.

📐 Architect's Scale IMPERIAL

The architect's scale is a triangular ruler with 11 different scales on its 6 edges. It's used for architectural drawings measured in feet and inches.

👀
Key feature: Each end of a scale reads in opposite directions. The numbers go 0→large on one end and large→0 on the other, sharing the same edge. Always find the "0" mark for your scale first!
Interactive Architect's Scale — ¼" = 1'-0" (hover over marks)
How to Read the Architect's Scale — Step by Step ▼
  1. Identify the scale you need (e.g., ¼" = 1'-0")
  2. Find that scale printed on the ruler edge
  3. Locate the zero (0) mark — this is your starting point
  4. The numbered marks show feet (1, 2, 3…). Each space between numbers = 1 foot at that scale
  5. The small divided section beyond the "0" shows inches (subdivided into 12 parts)
  6. Place the 0 at one end of your line and read the feet, then add any inch fractions
⚠️
Common mistake: Reading the wrong scale! Each edge has TWO scales. The smaller-numbered scale reads left-to-right; the larger reads right-to-left. Always confirm which direction your "0" is.
📏 Engineer's Scale IMPERIAL CIVIL

The civil engineer's scale uses ratio-based divisions: 10, 20, 30, 40, 50, and 60. These represent "1 inch = X feet" and are used for site plans, maps, and civil drawings.

Scale "10" means…

1 inch = 10 feet. Each numbered division = 1 foot. Used for large detail drawings.

Scale "50" means…

1 inch = 50 feet. Used for medium-sized site plans and subdivision layouts.

💡
Easy trick: The number on the engineer's scale IS the scale factor. Scale "40" = 1:480 (40 × 12). Just multiply the reading by the scale number to get feet.
📏 Metric Scale Rule METRIC

The metric scale rule (also triangular) has scales like 1:20, 1:25, 1:50, 1:75, 1:100, 1:125. The divisions are already in millimetres — much easier to calculate!

Reading 1:50

Each 1mm on the ruler = 50mm in reality. So if you measure 40mm on a 1:50 drawing, the real size is 40 × 50 = 2000mm = 2 metres.

Reading 1:100

Each 1mm = 100mm = 0.1m. Measure 55mm on a 1:100 plan? Real size = 55 × 100 = 5500mm = 5.5m.

💻 Digital Scale in CAD CAD / DIGITAL

In CAD software (AutoCAD, Revit, SketchUp), you always draw at 1:1 (full size). Scale is only applied when you set up your viewport or print layout.

Model Space

Always real-world size. A 6m wall is drawn as 6000mm. You never shrink objects in model space.

Paper Space / Layout

This is where you set your scale. A viewport set to 1:50 will show your 6m wall as 120mm on the printed sheet.

⚠️
Important: Even in CAD, you must understand scale to choose the right viewport scale, annotate with correct text heights, and specify dimension scales correctly.
